The Core Problem

Section 1 ‚Äî What this paper solves

Exchange rate movements do not fully pass through into import prices ‚Äî this is the incomplete ERPT phenomenon. This matters for monetary policy, inflation dynamics, and international shock transmission. The paper addresses three intertwined gaps in the literature:

üîó
Gap 1: Endogenous Linkages
Most open-economy DSGE models assume a small open economy where foreign variables are exogenous. This ignores feedback ‚Äî domestic shocks affecting trade partners and vice versa. Razafindrabe relaxes this by modeling N countries of different sizes that are endogenously linked through trade.
üìâ
Gap 2: Incomplete ERPT Mechanism
The paper embeds incomplete ERPT via nominal import price rigidity (Calvo pricing by importing firms), following Monacelli (2005). This creates a law-of-one-price gap that generates a new monetary policy trade-off between output stabilization and l.o.p gap stabilization.
üåç
Gap 3: Steady-State Estimation
Instead of using ad-hoc statistical filters (HP filter) which are a "black box", the paper uses a Global VAR to estimate steady states via long-horizon forecasts, capturing cointegration between domestic and foreign variables plus common global factors like oil prices.
üí±
Gap 4: Bilateral vs. Multilateral ERPT
The multi-country specification allows distinguishing between pass-through from bilateral exchange rate changes versus effective (multilateral) exchange rate changes, highlighting the role of currency invoicing and trade partner market share.

Model Features

Section 2 ‚Äî Architecture of the DSGE

Economies & Agents

The world consists of $N$ open economies indexed by $i \in \{1, \dots, N\}$, differing in population size, degree of openness, and international portfolio diversification. The Euro-area is the numeraire country. In the empirical application: Euro-area, UK, China, US, Japan, Switzerland.

üè≠
Domestic Firms
Monopolistic, Calvo pricing, single good, labor-only production
üö¢
Importing Firms
Buy homogeneous goods abroad, sell to domestic consumers with Calvo price setting ‚Üí source of incomplete ERPT
üë§
Households
Monopolistic labor supply, Calvo wages, external habit, Cobb-Douglas consumption over home & foreign goods
üè¶
Central Bank
Taylor-type rule responding to inflation, output gap, l.o.p gap, and their changes

Key Model Features

  • Multi-country (not small open economy): $N$ countries with asymmetric trade flows captured by import share matrix $M$ with elements $\alpha_{ij}$
  • Incomplete ERPT via Calvo import pricing: importing firms set prices in staggered fashion ‚Üí law-of-one-price gap $\Psi_{it}$
  • Home consumption bias: $\max_{i \neq j} \alpha_{ij} < \alpha_{ii} < 1$
  • External habit formation: $H_{it} = h C_{it-1}$, generating real exchange rate volatility and persistence
  • Monopolistic wage setting √† la Calvo with indexation to past CPI inflation
  • Complete international asset markets ‚Üí risk sharing and UIP conditions
  • Seven structural shocks per country: exchange rate, technology, monetary policy, inflation targeting, wage markup, price markup, preference
  • GVAR-based steady-state estimation capturing global interdependencies, cointegration, and oil prices as a common global factor

Model & Estimation Workflow

Section 3 ‚Äî From theory to empirical results
Step 1 ‚Äî Theoretical Model
Derive Multi-Country DSGE Equilibrium
For each country $i$: solve household optimization (Euler, wage setting), domestic firm pricing (Calvo), importing firm pricing (Calvo ‚Üí l.o.p gap), market clearing. Combine risk sharing + UIP + trade structure.
‚ñº
Step 2 ‚Äî Canonical Representation
Reduce to 4 Core Equations + Monetary Rule
(i) New Keynesian Phillips Curve (domestic inflation), (ii) Import price inflation law of motion, (iii) Dynamic IS equation (matrix form linking outputs via $M$), (iv) Monetary policy rule with l.o.p gap. This preserves tractability despite the multi-country complexity.
‚ñº
Step 3 ‚Äî GVAR Steady-State Estimation
Estimate Steady States via Global VAR Long-Horizon Forecasts
Estimate a cointegrating GVAR with trade-weighted foreign variables. Decompose each variable into permanent (deterministic trend + Beveridge-Nelson stochastic trend) and transitory components. Steady state = permanent component. Variables: output, interest rate, PPI inflation, IPI inflation, exchange rate, real wage, oil price.
‚ñº
Step 4 ‚Äî Compute Deviations
Observable Variables as Deviations from GVAR Steady States
$\hat{x}_t = x_t - \bar{x}_t$ where $\bar{x}_t$ is the GVAR-estimated steady state (long-horizon forecast). This replaces HP-filtering or linear detrending with an economically interpretable alternative.
‚ñº
Step 5 ‚Äî Bayesian Estimation
Estimate Structural Parameters via Metropolis-Hastings
Parameters estimated in 3 blocks (behavioral, nominal frictions, monetary policy) to manage dimensionality. 100,000 draws per block. Priors based on Smets & Wouters (2003), Adolfson et al. (2007). Calibrated: $\beta = 0.99$, import shares $\alpha_{ij}$ from trade data (1999‚Äì2011 averages).
‚ñº
Step 6 ‚Äî Analysis
IRFs, Variance Decomposition, ERPT Analysis
Impulse responses to exchange rate shocks (bilateral vs. multilateral), monetary policy, markup, preference, inflation targeting shocks. Conditional variance decomposition of Euro-area import price inflation over 8 quarters. Robustness: pre-crisis subsample, linear detrending benchmark.

Equation Inventory

Section 4 ‚Äî Complete list of model equations

A. Exchange Rates & Terms of Trade

Bilateral Nominal Exchange Rate (log-linear)
$$\hat{e}_{ijt} = \hat{e}_{it} - \hat{e}_{jt}$$
Country 1 (Euro-area) is the numeraire, so $\hat{e}_{1t} = 0$.
Effective Real Exchange Rate
$$q_{it} = \left(\hat{e}_{it} - \hat{p}_{it}\right) - \sum_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_{ij}\left(\hat{e}_{jt} - \hat{p}_{jt}\right)$$
$\hat{p}_{it}$ = CPI of country $i$; $\alpha_{ij}$ = import share of goods from $j$ in $i$'s consumption basket.
Bilateral Terms of Trade
$$\hat{s}_{ijt} = \hat{p}_{ijt} - \hat{p}^d_{it}$$
Price of imports from $j$ relative to domestic PPI.
Effective Terms of Trade
$$\hat{s}_{it} = \hat{p}^f_{it} - (1 - \alpha_{ii})\hat{p}^d_{it} = \hat{p}_{it} - \hat{p}^d_{it}$$
CPI‚ÄìPPI‚ÄìIPI Inflation Link
$$\hat{\pi}_{it} = \hat{\pi}^f_{it} + \alpha_{ii}\hat{\pi}^d_{it}$$
Aggregate Import Inflation
$$\hat{\pi}^f_{it} = \sum_{\substack{j=1 \\ j \neq i}}^{N} \alpha_{ij}\hat{\pi}^{jf}_{it}$$

B. Incomplete Pass-Through & Law-of-One-Price Gap

Bilateral L.O.P. Gap
$$\psi_{ij,t} = \hat{e}_{ijt} + \hat{p}^d_{jt} - \hat{p}_{ijt}$$
Measures deviation between foreign prices (at the dock) and domestic-currency import prices.
Effective L.O.P. Gap
$$\Psi_{it} = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_{ij}\psi_{ij,t} = q_{it} - \sum_{j=1}^{N}\alpha_{ij}\hat{s}_{jt}$$
Two sources of PPP deviation: relative price variations (terms of trade) and l.o.p deviations.
CPI‚ÄìPPI Relationship (matrix form)
$$\hat{e}_t - \hat{p}_t = M\left(\hat{e}_t - \hat{p}^d_t\right) + \Psi_t$$
$M$ is the $N \times N$ import share matrix.
Real Exchange Rate in Terms of L.O.P. Gap
$$q_t = (I_N - M)\,M\left(\hat{e}_t - \hat{p}^d_t\right) + (I_N - M)\,\Psi_t$$

C. International Risk Sharing & UIP

International Risk Sharing Condition
$$\hat{c}_{it} - h\hat{c}_{it-1} = \hat{c}_{jt} - h\hat{c}_{jt-1} + (1-h)\hat{q}_{ijt}$$
Under complete markets. Incomplete ERPT distorts this via its impact on the real exchange rate.
Uncovered Interest Parity
$$E_t\left\{\Delta\hat{e}_{ijt+1}\right\} = \hat{r}^n_{it} - \hat{r}^n_{jt}$$

D. Households

Consumption Index (Cobb-Douglas)
$$C_{it} = \frac{\prod_{j=1}^{N} C_{ijt}^{\alpha_{ij}}}{\prod_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_{ij}^{\alpha_{ij}}}$$
Euler Equation (log-linearized)
$$\hat{c}_{it} = \frac{h}{1+h}\hat{c}_{it-1} + \frac{1}{1+h}E_t\{\hat{c}_{it+1}\} - \frac{1-h}{1+h}\left(\hat{r}^n_{it} - E_t\{\hat{\pi}_{it+1}\}\right)$$
Wage Markup / Labor Market Friction
$$\hat{\mu}^w_{it} = \hat{w}_{it} - \hat{p}_{it} - \psi\hat{l}_{it} - \frac{1}{1-h}\left(\hat{c}_{it} - h\hat{c}_{it-1}\right)$$
Difference between real wage and marginal rate of substitution.
Real Wage Law of Motion
$$\widehat{wr}_{it} = \frac{1}{1+\beta}\widehat{wr}_{it-1} + \frac{\beta}{1+\beta}E_t\{\widehat{wr}_{it+1}\} + \frac{\gamma^w_i}{1+\beta}\hat{\pi}_{it-1} - \frac{1+\beta\gamma^w_i}{1+\beta}\hat{\pi}_{it} + \frac{\beta}{1+\beta}E_t\{\hat{\pi}_{it+1}\} - \delta^w_i\hat{\mu}^w_{it} + \delta^w_i\varepsilon^w_{it}$$
where $\delta^w_i = \frac{(1-\theta_{iW})(1-\beta\theta_{iW})}{(1+\beta)\theta_{iW}k_w}$

E. Domestic Firms

Production Function
$$Y_{it}(f_i) = A_{it}L_{it}(f_i)$$
Labor-only with aggregate technology $A_{it}$; $\hat{a}_{it} = \rho_{a_i}\hat{a}_{it-1} + \varepsilon^a_{it}$
Real Marginal Cost (deflated by PPI)
$$\widehat{mc}_{it} = \hat{w}_{it} - \hat{p}^d_{it} - \hat{a}_{it}$$
Marginal Cost (substituted)
$$\widehat{mc}_{it} = \frac{1}{1-h}\left[(1+\psi)(1-h)\hat{y}_{it} - h\hat{y}_{it-1} - h\Phi_{it}\right] + \hat{\mu}^w_{it} - (1+\psi)\hat{a}_{it}$$
$\Phi_{it}$ = effective real interest rate differential, defined below.
Domestic Inflation Dynamics (Phillips Curve input)
$$\hat{\pi}^d_{it} = \frac{\gamma^d_i}{1+\gamma^d_i\beta}\hat{\pi}^d_{it-1} + \frac{\beta}{1+\gamma^d_i\beta}E_t\{\hat{\pi}^d_{it+1}\} + \lambda^d_i\left(\widehat{mc}_{it} + \varepsilon^p_{it}\right)$$
where $\lambda^d_i = \frac{(1-\beta\theta_{iH})(1-\theta_{iH})}{(1+\gamma^d_i\beta)\theta_{iH}}$

F. Importing Firms

Bilateral Import Inflation (Calvo)
$$\hat{\pi}^{jf}_{it} = \frac{\gamma^f_i}{1+\gamma^f_i\beta}\hat{\pi}^{jf}_{it-1} + \frac{\beta}{1+\gamma^f_i\beta}E_t\{\hat{\pi}^{jf}_{it+1}\} + \lambda^f_i\left(\psi_{ij,t} + \varepsilon^p_{jt}\right)$$
where $\lambda^f_i = \frac{(1-\beta\theta_{iF})(1-\theta_{iF})}{(1+\gamma^f_i\beta)\theta_{iF}}$. The l.o.p gap acts as the importing firm's marginal cost.

G. Canonical Representation (4 + 1 Core Equations)

Eq. (14) ‚Äî New Keynesian Phillips Curve
$$\hat{\pi}^d_{it} = \frac{\gamma^d_i}{1+\gamma^d_i\beta}\hat{\pi}^d_{it-1} + \frac{\beta}{1+\gamma^d_i\beta}E_t\{\hat{\pi}^d_{it+1}\} + \omega_i\hat{y}_{it} - \frac{\lambda^d_i h}{1-h}\hat{y}_{it-1} - \frac{\lambda^d_i h}{1-h}\Phi_{it} + \lambda^d_i\hat{\mu}^w_{it} - \lambda^d_i(1+\psi)\hat{a}_{it} + \lambda^d_i\varepsilon^p_{it}$$
where $\omega_i = \lambda^d_i(1+\psi(1-h))/(1-h)$
Eq. (15) ‚Äî Import Price Inflation Law of Motion
$$\hat{\pi}^f_{it} = \frac{\gamma^f_i}{1+\gamma^f_i\beta}\hat{\pi}^f_{it-1} + \frac{\beta}{1+\gamma^f_i\beta}E_t\{\hat{\pi}^f_{it+1}\} + \lambda^f_i\left(\Psi_{it} + \varepsilon^{fp}_{it}\right)$$
where $\varepsilon^{fp}_{it} = \sum_{j \neq i}\alpha_{ij}\varepsilon^p_{jt}$ = weighted foreign cost-push shocks (endogenous in multi-country setting).
Eq. (16) ‚Äî Dynamic IS Equation (matrix form)
$$M\hat{y}_t = \frac{1}{1+h}ME_t\{\hat{y}_{t+1}\} + \frac{h}{1+h}M\hat{y}_{t-1} + \frac{1-h}{1+h}E_t\{\Delta\Psi_{t+1}\} - \frac{h}{1+h}ME_t\{\Delta\Phi_{t+1}\} - \frac{1-h}{1+h}\left(\hat{r}^n_t - E_t\{\hat{\pi}_{t+1}\}\right)$$
Outputs linked through the import share matrix $M$. L.o.p gap enters ‚Üí trade-off with output gap.
Eq. (17) ‚Äî Monetary Policy Rule
$$\hat{r}^n_{it} = \rho_{ir}\hat{r}^n_{it-1} + (1-\rho_{ir})\left[\bar{\pi}_{it} + r_{\pi_i}(\hat{\pi}_{it-1} - \bar{\pi}_{it}) + r_{y_i}\tilde{y}_{it} + r_{\psi_i}\Psi_{it}\right] + r_{\Delta\pi_i}\Delta\hat{\pi}_{it} + r_{\Delta y_i}\Delta\tilde{y}_{it} + \varepsilon^r_{it}$$
$\bar{\pi}_{it} = \rho_{\pi_i}\bar{\pi}_{it-1} + \eta^{\pi_i}_t$ is the time-varying inflation target; $r_{\psi_i}$ captures the l.o.p gap trade-off.

H. Supporting Equations

Goods Market Clearing & Output-Consumption Link
$$\hat{y}_{it} - h\hat{y}_{it-1} = (\hat{c}_{it} - h\hat{c}_{it-1}) + (1-h)\hat{s}_{it} + h\Phi_{it}$$
Effective Real Interest Rate Differential
$$\Phi_{it} = \sum_{j=1}^{N}\varkappa_{ij}\left[(\hat{r}^n_{it-1} - \hat{\pi}^d_{it}) - (\hat{r}^n_{jt-1} - \hat{\pi}_{jt})\right]$$
$\varkappa_{ij}$ = share of country $i$'s output exported to $j$. Habit formation increases output volatility via this channel.
Natural Output (flexible-price equilibrium)
$$\hat{y}^n_{it} = \hat{a}_{it} + \frac{h}{1+\psi(1-h)}\left(\hat{y}^n_{it-1} - \hat{a}_{it}\right)$$
Consumption‚ÄìOutput Link (matrix form)
$$\hat{c}_t - h\hat{c}_{t-1} = M(\hat{y}_t - h\hat{y}_{t-1}) + (1-h)\Psi_t - hM\Phi_t$$
Aggregate Output‚ÄìRelative Prices (matrix form)
$$(I_N - M)(\hat{y}_t - h\hat{y}_{t-1}) = (1-h)(I_N - M)(\hat{e}_t - \hat{p}^d_t) + h(I_N - M)\Phi_t$$

Section 5 ‚Äî Bayesian estimation & GVAR

Step A: GVAR Steady-State Estimation

The Global VAR error correction representation:

GVAR Error Correction Form
$$G\Delta x_t = [a - \alpha\beta']\,x_{t-1} - \gamma(t-1) + \sum_{i=1}^{p-1}\Gamma_i\Delta x_{t-i} + u_t$$
$G$ = link matrix encoding trade weights $\omega_{ij}$; $x_t$ = global vector of all country-specific variables.
Beveridge-Nelson Stochastic Trend (Permanent Component)
$$x^P_{s,t} = C(1)\sum_{j=1}^{t}\epsilon_j$$
$C(1) = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}C_i$ from the VAR(p) companion form. The transitory component $\hat{x}^T_t$ is obtained as OLS residuals from regressing $z_t = x_t - x^P_{s,t}$ on a constant and linear trend.
Country-specific GVAR variables: For UK, China, Japan, Switzerland: domestic = $(y_{it}, r^n_{it}, \pi^d_{it}, \pi^f_{it}, e_{it}, wr_{it})$; foreign weakly exogenous = $(y^*_{it}, r^{n,*}_{it}, wr^*_{it}, p^{oil}_t)$. For the US, oil price is endogenous. For the Euro-area, the exchange rate enters the foreign block.

Impulse Response Findings

  • Import price inflation response to exchange rate depreciation is hump-shaped, peaking after ~2 years ‚Äî nominal rigidity creates persistent but delayed pass-through
  • Upon impact, import price inflation rises by 0.06%; cumulative after 2 years ‚âà 0.20%
  • Bilateral ERPT differs by currency: pound generates the largest response, Swiss franc the smallest
  • As $\theta_F \to 1$ (high rigidity), ERPT ‚Üí 0 (mimics LCP-PTM); as $\theta_F \to 0$, ERPT ‚Üí complete (mimics PCP)
  • Monetary policy response to l.o.p gap ($r_\psi$) is low ‚Äî reflecting the output gap vs. l.o.p gap trade-off

Section 7 ‚Äî Bridging to Indonesian regional disaster DSGE

Key Transferable Elements

1. Multi-region structure ‚Üí replace countries with Indonesian regions. The import share matrix $M$ with elements $\alpha_{ij}$ becomes an interregional trade linkage matrix. Your disaster-affected and non-affected regions are endogenously linked just as the Euro-area and its trade partners are here.

2. Calvo nominal rigidity ‚Üí adaptation for disaster-induced supply disruption. The Calvo parameter $\theta_F$ controls the speed of price adjustment. In your context, you can introduce a disaster shock that temporarily increases the fraction of firms unable to adjust production/prices, or destroys capital stock.

3. L.o.p gap concept ‚Üí regional price divergence after disaster. Post-disaster supply disruptions create regional price divergences analogous to the law-of-one-price gap. The trade-off between output stabilization and l.o.p gap stabilization maps onto the policy trade-off between reconstruction spending and inflation control.

4. GVAR steady-state estimation ‚Üí applicable for Indonesian regional data. If you have provincial GDP, prices, and trade flows, you can estimate a regional VAR to get economically interpretable steady states rather than HP-filtering.

5. Bayesian estimation by blocks is directly transferable for managing the dimensionality of a multi-region model.

6. Variance decomposition lets you answer: "What fraction of regional output/consumption volatility is driven by the disaster shock vs. policy shocks vs. interregional spillovers?"
