Section 1

The Core Problem

Weather-related natural disasters (cyclones, floods, storms) are becoming more frequent and more devastating, especially for small states and low-income countries. The paper asks: do differences in disaster exposure alone explain significant divergence in growth, fiscal positions, and welfare between disaster-prone and non-disaster-prone developing countries?

Why Existing Models Fail
  • Linearization is inadequate ‚Äî disasters can destroy up to 260% of GDP (Hurricane Maria in Dominica, 2017); linear approximations break down.
  • Deterministic steady states are unrealistic ‚Äî agents facing recurring disasters should have a stochastic steady state where disaster risk itself shifts long-run equilibrium levels.
  • Perfect foresight misses risk channels ‚Äî precautionary savings, uncertainty premia, and forward-looking behavior under disaster risk are absent in deterministic simulations.
Key Empirical Facts (EM-DAT 1998‚Äì2017)
  • Disaster-prone countries: annual probability 16.2% per 1000 km¬≤, avg. damage 6.65% of GDP
  • Non-disaster-prone: annual probability 0.28%, avg. damage 0.52% of GDP
  • 74% of disaster-prone countries are small economies (small states or LICs)
  • Probability rose from 13.8% ‚Üí 18.7% between the two decades; damages from 4.7% ‚Üí 8.6% of GDP
Central Research Questions
(1) How much do natural disaster shocks ‚Äî frequency & magnitude ‚Äî weigh on growth, fiscal outcomes, and welfare of disaster-prone developing countries?
(2) Does climate change (shifting the distribution) amplify divergence?
(3) Are ex-post grants or ex-ante resilient infrastructure more cost-effective?
Section 2

Model Features ‚Äî DSGE Architecture

The model is a small-open-economy real business cycle (RBC) model with stochastic trend growth, disaster shocks √† la Gourio (2012), and extensions for public investment, external debt, resilient infrastructure, and international aid.

Households

Epstein‚ÄìZin recursive preferences separating risk aversion ($\gamma$) from IES ($1/\hat\psi$). Precautionary savings channel from disaster risk. Consume, supply labor, invest in private capital, hold domestic bonds.

Firms

Cobb‚ÄìDouglas production with private & public capital. Stochastic trend TFP with drift. Disasters destroy capital and reduce TFP ‚Äî capturing permanent level effects (no overshooting), consistent with Hsiang & Jina (2014).

Government

Consumption tax, external debt, public investment (standard + resilient infrastructure). Tax rule responds to debt. Public investment reacts to disasters (reconstruction). Sovereign risk premium on external debt.

Disaster Shocks

Binary i.i.d. shock $d_t \in \{0,1\}$ with probability $p_d$. Size $\theta_t$ follows a persistent AR(1) with stochastic volatility. Destroys fraction of private capital, public capital, and TFP permanently (level shift).

Resilient Capital

Fraction of public capital stock immune to disasters. More expensive by factor $(1+\iota)$. Donors can finance share $\vartheta$ of extra cost. Trade-off: protection vs. fiscal burden.

International Aid

Ex-post grants disbursed after disasters (rule-based on disaster magnitude). Ex-ante grants financing resilient capital. The paper compares their cost-effectiveness.

No Nominal Rigidities
Unlike Fern√°ndez-Villaverde & Levintal (2018), this model abstracts from monetary policy ‚Äî the focus is long-run real effects of disasters. No price stickiness, no Taylor rule.

5

6
Compare & Compute Welfare Percent differences in simulation averages (DP vs. NDP). Consumption-equivalent welfare loss $\omega$ via Eq. (27).
Section 4

Key Equations

4.1 Household ‚Äî Recursive Preferences

Eq. (1) ‚Äî Epstein‚ÄìZin Value Function
$$V_t^{1-\psi} = U_t^{1-\psi} + \beta E_t\left(V_{t+1}^{1-\gamma}\right)^{\frac{1-\psi}{1-\gamma}}$$
$U_t = c_t(1-l_t)^\nu$ is period utility. $\gamma$ = risk aversion, $\hat\psi = 1-(1+\nu)(1-\psi)$ governs IES = $1/\hat\psi$. Preference for early resolution of uncertainty when $\gamma > \hat\psi$.
Eq. (2) ‚Äî Household Budget Constraint
$$(1+\tau_t)c_t + x_t + b_{t+1} = w_t l_t + r_t k_t + R_{t-1}b_t + T_t + \Theta$$
$\tau_t$ = consumption tax, $x_t$ = private investment, $b_t$ = domestic bonds, $T_t$ = transfers, $\Theta$ = remittances.

4.2 Private Capital & Disaster Shocks

Eq. (3)‚Äì(4) ‚Äî Capital Accumulation with Disaster Destruction
$$k_t^* = (1-\delta)k_t + \left(1 - S\!\left[\frac{x_t}{x_{t-1}}\right]\right)x_t$$ $$\log k_t = \log k_{t-1}^* - d_t\theta_t$$
$k_t^*$ = optimal capital after investment; $k_t$ = actual capital after disaster. $d_t \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p_d)$. If $d_t=1$, capital suffers permanent one-off depreciation $\theta_t$.
Eq. (5) ‚Äî Disaster Size Process (Stochastic Volatility)
$$\log\theta_t = (1-\rho_\theta)\log\bar\theta + \rho_\theta\log\theta_{t-1} + \sigma_\theta\epsilon_{\theta,t}$$
$\bar\theta$ = mean disaster size (calibrated to 6.65% GDP loss for DP countries). $\rho_\theta = 0.9$ persistence. $\sigma_\theta$ matches dispersion of damages.

4.3 Household First-Order Conditions

Eq. (6) ‚Äî Euler Equation
$$1 = E_t M_{t+1} R_t$$
$M_{t+1} = \beta\frac{\lambda_{t+1}}{\lambda_t} \frac{V_{t+1}^{\psi-\gamma}}{E_t(V_{t+1}^{1-\gamma})^{(\psi-\gamma)/(1-\gamma)}}$ is the EZ stochastic discount factor.
Eq. (8) ‚Äî Asset Price (Tobin's q)
$$q_t = E_t\!\left(M_{t+1}e^{-d_{t+1}\theta_{t+1}}\left[r_{t+1}+q_{t+1}(1-\delta)\right]\right)$$
Disaster shock enters the capital pricing equation directly via $e^{-d_{t+1}\theta_{t+1}}$.

4.4 Production & TFP

Eq. (11) ‚Äî Cobb‚ÄìDouglas Production Function
$$y_{i,t} = A_t\left(k_{i,t}^{1-\alpha_g}\bar{k}_{g,t}^{\alpha_g}\right)^\alpha l_{i,t}^{1-\alpha}$$
$\alpha$ = total capital share (0.32), $\alpha_g$ = public capital share (0.22). Public capital $\bar{k}_{g,t}$ enters as externality.
Eq. (12) ‚Äî TFP Process with Disaster Hit
$$\log A_t = \log A_{t-1} + \Lambda_A + z_{A,t} - (1-\alpha)\,d_t\theta_t$$
Disasters reduce TFP by $(1-\alpha)\theta_t$ to ensure output falls by $\theta_t$ (same as capital ‚Äî see Appendix D). $\Lambda_A = 0.0035$ quarterly trend growth. No overshooting: growth rate reverts but level is permanently lower.

4.5 Government Sector

Eq. (16)‚Äì(19) ‚Äî Public Capital: Standard + Resilient
$$\bar{k}_{g,t} = k_{g,t} + k_{ga,t-1}$$ $$k_{g,t}^* = (1-\delta_g)k_{g,t} + x_{g,t}, \qquad \log k_{g,t} = \log k_{g,t-1}^* - d_t\theta_t$$ $$k_{ga,t} = (1-\delta_g)k_{ga,t-1} + x_{ga,t}$$
Standard public capital $k_{g,t}$ is destroyed like private capital. Resilient capital $k_{ga,t}$ is immune. They are perfect substitutes in production.
Eq. (20) ‚Äî Public Investment Reconstruction Rule
$$\log\!\left(\frac{\tilde{x}_{g,t}}{\tilde{x}_g}\right) = \rho_{xg}\log\!\left(\frac{\tilde{x}_{g,t-1}}{\tilde{x}_g}\right) + \rho_{xd}\left(\frac{d_t\theta_t}{\bar{d}\bar\theta}\right)$$
$\rho_{xg}=0.95$ (inertia), $\rho_{xd}=1.5$ (responsiveness to disasters). Captures post-disaster reconstruction spending.
Eq. (21) ‚Äî Government Budget Constraint
$$b_{g,t} = R_{t-1}^* b_{g,t-1} + g + x_{g,t} + [1+(1-\vartheta)\iota]\,x_{ga,t} - \tau_t^c c_t + T_t - \phi_t$$
$\vartheta\in[0,1]$ = donor share of resilient capital extra cost $\iota$. $\phi_t$ = grants from donors.
Eq. (22) ‚Äî Tax Rule
$$\log\!\left(\frac{\tau_t^c}{\tau^c}\right) = \rho_\tau\log\!\left(\frac{\tau_{t-1}^c}{\tau^c}\right) + \rho_{\tau b}\log\!\left(\frac{\tilde{b}_{g,t}}{\tilde{b}_g}\right)$$
Eq. (24) ‚Äî Sovereign Risk Premium
$$R_t^* = R\,e^{\eta\left(\frac{b_{g,t}}{b_g}-1\right)}$$
$\eta=0$ in baseline; $\eta=0.01$ in risk-premium exercises (calibrated to Jamaica T-bill response).
Eq. (23) ‚Äî Post-Disaster Grant Rule
$$\log\!\left(\frac{\tilde\phi_t}{\tilde\phi}\right) = \rho_\phi\log\!\left(\frac{\tilde\phi_{t-1}}{\tilde\phi}\right) + (1-\rho_\phi)\,\rho_{\phi d}\left(\frac{d_t\theta_t}{\bar{d}\bar\theta}\right)$$
$\rho_\phi$ = persistence of disbursement; $\rho_{\phi d}$ = sensitivity to disaster magnitude.

4.6 Welfare Metric

Eq. (27) ‚Äî Consumption-Equivalent Welfare Loss
$$\bar{V}^{\text{NDP}}\!\left\{(1-\omega)\,c_t^{\text{NDP}},\, l_t^{\text{NDP}}\right\} = \bar{V}^{\text{DP}}\!\left\{c_t^{\text{DP}},\, l_t^{\text{DP}}\right\}$$
$\omega\times 100$ = permanent % consumption loss NDP households must suffer to be as well off as DP households. Baseline: $\omega=5.3\%$.

4.7 Market Clearing & Balance of Payments

Eq. (25) ‚Äî Resource Constraint
$$y_t = c_t + x_t + g + x_{g,t} + [1+(1-\vartheta)\iota]\,x_{ga,t} + nx_t$$
Eq. (26) ‚Äî Balance of Payments
$$-(b_{g,t}-b_{g,t-1}) = nx_t + \phi_t - (R_{t-1}^*-1)b_{g,t-1} + \Theta$$
Section 5

Solution Method ‚Äî Taylor Projection

The model is solved using third-order Taylor projection (Levintal 2018; Fern√°ndez-Villaverde & Levintal 2018), which combines:

How Taylor Projection Works
  • Grid from projection methods ‚Äî approximate the policy function at multiple points of the state space (like Smolyak collocation)
  • Taylor expansions ‚Äî at each grid point, use Taylor series (up to 3rd order) to solve the local system of expectations equations
  • Result: a semi-global solution accurate for large nonlinearities ‚Äî much better than perturbation around a single point
Why Not Perturbation?
  • 1st-order perturbation: max Euler errors as bad as $-0.1$ (severely inaccurate)
  • 3rd-order perturbation: mean Euler errors $-1.8$ to $-2.5$ (still poor)
  • 2nd‚Äì3rd order Taylor projection: mean Euler errors $-3.6$ to $-6.9$ (solidly accurate)
  • Also faster than 5th-order perturbation and 3rd-order Smolyak collocation
Why This Matters for Disaster Models
Natural disaster shocks are large and rare ‚Äî exactly the case where linearization fails catastrophically. The model needs to capture how a 25% GDP shock (Hurricane Matthew) propagates differently from a 6.65% average shock. Taylor projection handles this nonlinearity accurately.

IRF Insights

An average disaster (6.65% of GDP) causes output to fall sharply, with recovery taking about 3 years ‚Äî but the level of GDP is permanently lower (no overshooting in TFP growth). Section 8

Policy Analysis

Ex-Post: Post-Disaster Grants
  • Welfare gains are monotonically increasing in grant size
  • To eliminate the 5.3% welfare loss: need avg. yearly grant of 7.4% of GDP (~$602M for avg. DP country) ‚Äî far exceeds actual aid
  • Grant timing matters less than size (discounting favors immediate disbursement but effect is second-order)
  • Actual post-Hurricane Matthew aid (IMF RCF $41.6M + US $105M) = tiny fraction of what's needed
Ex-Ante: Resilient Infrastructure
  • Self-financing resilience yields small welfare gains (extra cost $\iota=50\%$ generates fiscal burden)
  • If donors finance 100% of extra cost ($\vartheta=1$): welfare loss eliminated at 85% resilient share, costing yearly grant of 1.8% of GDP (~$143M)
  • This is less than a quarter of the ex-post grant needed ‚Äî ex-ante is ~4√ó more cost-effective
  • Even partial donor support ($\vartheta=0.5$) yields substantially better outcomes than ex-post grants alone
Main Policy Finding
International aid financing ex-ante resilient infrastructure is roughly 4 times more cost-effective than ex-post disaster relief grants. Donors need to disburse ~$143M/year for resilience vs. ~$602M/year for post-disaster elimination of welfare losses.

Section 10

Other Important Details

Stochastic vs. Deterministic Steady State

At the stochastic steady state (SSS), GDP of DP countries is already 2.3% lower than NDP ‚Äî purely from disaster risk, with no actual shocks hitting. This is the ex-ante channel: agents' forward-looking behavior shifts equilibrium. The SSS is obtained by simulating 100 quarters without shocks from the deterministic SS.

Two Channels of Disaster Effects

Disaster risk (ex-ante): shifts stochastic steady state, induces precautionary savings, affects asset prices even without shocks. Disaster strikes (ex-post): actual realizations destroy capital/TFP, trigger reconstruction, raise debt and taxes. Both contribute to the 5.3% welfare loss.

Detrending

Variables detrended by $z_t = A_t^{1/(1-\alpha)}$ to achieve stationarity. Growth rates: $\hat{z}_t = z_t/z_{t-1}$. The full stationary system (35 equations) is in Appendix C.

Robustness

Results survive: alternative disaster distributions (Barro-style 1% GDP threshold), different fiscal rule parameters ($\rho_{\tau b}$, $\rho_\tau$), different public investment responsiveness ($\rho_{xd}$), doubled public depreciation rate, lower disaster risk persistence ($\rho_\theta=0.5$). GDP growth gap robust to second decimal digit.
