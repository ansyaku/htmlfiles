01

Problem Statement

Small developing states sit at the intersection of extreme natural disaster vulnerability and severe fiscal constraints. Cyclone Pam (2015) inflicted damages equivalent to 60.8% of Vanuatu's GDP ‚Äî destroying public infrastructure, private capital, and crippling productivity. The core problem is a three-way tension:

üî¥ The Reconstruction Dilemma

Rebuilding public infrastructure swiftly (within ~6 years) is necessary to restore growth, but financing it through taxes crushes household consumption (VAT would spike from 12.5% ‚Üí 23%), while financing through borrowing pushes public debt above 90% of GDP, threatening debt sustainability.

üü° The Resilience Question

Could ex-ante investments in adaptation infrastructure (e.g., seawalls, climate-proofed roads) and fiscal buffers (contingency funds) have reduced damage severity and post-disaster fiscal stress? What is the optimal balance between prevention and reconstruction?

Research Questions Addressed

  • RQ1: How much grant financing must donors provide to ensure debt sustainability during accelerated reconstruction?
  • RQ2: If donors had supported pre-disaster resilience investments, how would Vanuatu's debt profile differ?
  • RQ3: What is the relative effectiveness of adaptation infrastructure vs. fiscal buffers vs. post-disaster grants?

Model Features

Model Type

A two-sector dynamic small open economy model with traded (tourism/agriculture) and nontraded goods sectors, featuring government fiscal policy, multiple financing instruments, and a rich natural disaster transmission mechanism.

Small Open Economy Two Sectors (T/NT) Deterministic Simulation Perfect Foresight Known Disaster Shock

The model features five distinct channels through which a natural disaster transmits to the macroeconomy:

1
Public Capital Destruction
Permanent damage to roads, ports, airports ‚Äî the backbone of economic activity.
2
Private Capital Destruction
Permanent damage to factories, housing, private productive assets.
3
Productivity Losses
Temporary TFP decline from disruption ‚Äî debris clearing, workforce displacement, crop destruction.
4
Investment Inefficiency
Post-disaster reconstruction suffers from moral hazard, poorly managed projects, and aid misallocation.
5
Creditworthiness Damage
Sovereign credit rating deterioration raises borrowing costs on external commercial debt.

Household Structure

  • Ricardian households ‚Äî optimize intertemporally, invest in private capital, hold bonds
  • Liquidity-constrained households (12.7%) ‚Äî hand-to-mouth, consume entire disposable income
  • CES consumption basket over traded, imported, and nontraded goods

Government Instruments

  • Standard infrastructure investment \(i_{zi}\)
  • Adaptation infrastructure investment \(i_{za}\)
  • External contingency fund \(\mathfrak{s}\)
  • Financing: VAT, domestic debt, concessional debt, commercial external debt, grants

Production Structure

  • Cobb-Douglas production in each sector combining public capital, private capital, and labor
  • Public capital is a CES aggregate of standard infrastructure \(z^i\) and adaptation capital \(z^a\)
  • Adaptation capital simultaneously enters production AND reduces disaster damages

Financial Architecture

  • Concessional debt (zero real interest)
  • External commercial debt with endogenous risk premium
  • Domestic bonds
  • External private debt with premium over sovereign rate
  • Portfolio adjustment costs √† la Schmitt-Groh√© & Uribe (2003)

List of Key Equations

4.1 ‚Äî Firms

Eq. (1) ‚Äî Firm Profit $$\Phi_{j,t} = P_{j,t}\left(1 - \frac{D_{j,t}}{(1+\pi_j z^a_{t-1})^{\nu_D}}\right) y_{j,t} - w_t l_{j,t} - r_{j,t} k_{j,t-1}$$
Profits in sector \(j \in \{n, x\}\). Disasters reduce effective output; adaptation capital \(z^a\) mitigates via the denominator.
Eq. (2) ‚Äî Production Function $$y_{j,t} = A_{j,t}\, z_{t-1}^{\psi_j}\, k_{j,t-1}^{\alpha_j}\, l_{j,t}^{1-\alpha_j} \quad \text{for } j = n, x$$
Cobb-Douglas with public capital \(z\), private capital \(k\), and labor \(l\). Sector-specific TFP \(A_j\).
Eq. (3) ‚Äî CES Public Capital Aggregate $$z_t = \left[\rho_z^{1/\xi}\left(z^i_t\right)^{\frac{\xi-1}{\xi}} + (1-\rho_z)^{1/\xi}\left(\nu_a z^a_t\right)^{\frac{\xi-1}{\xi}}\right]^{\frac{\xi}{\xi-1}}$$
CES combination of standard (\(z^i\)) and adaptation (\(z^a\)) infrastructure. \(\xi\) controls substitutability: \(\xi \to \infty\) (perfect substitutes), \(\xi \to 1\) (Cobb-Douglas), \(\xi \to 0\) (Leontief).
Eqs. (4)‚Äì(5) ‚Äî Factor Demands $$P_{j,t}\,\alpha_j \frac{y_{j,t}}{k_{j,t-1}} = r_{j,t} \qquad\qquad P_{j,t}(1-\alpha_j)\frac{y_{j,t}}{l_{j,t}} = w_t$$
Standard first-order conditions: marginal product of capital = rental rate; marginal product of labor = wage.
Damage Decomposition $$\left(1 - D_{z_j}\right) = \left(\frac{1 - D_j}{(1+\pi_j z^a)^{\nu_D}}\right)^{\varpi_z}, \quad \left(1 - D_{k_j}\right) = \left(\frac{1 - D_j}{(1+\pi_j z^a)^{\nu_D}}\right)^{\varpi_k}$$
\(\varpi_z = 0.4\) and \(\varpi_k = 0.3\) control relative severity of damage to public vs. private capital. Residual goes to TFP losses.

4.2 ‚Äî Government

Eqs. (7)‚Äì(8) ‚Äî Public Capital Accumulation $$z^i_t = (1-\delta_{zi})\,z^i_{t-1} + (1-D_s)\,s_t\,i_{zi,t}$$ $$z^a_t = (1-\delta_{za})\,z^a_{t-1} + (1-D_s)\,s_t\,i_{za,t}$$
New capital = surviving old capital + efficient new investment. Efficiency \(s_t \in [0,1]\) is hit by \(D_s\) post-disaster. Adaptation depreciates slower: \(\delta_{za} = 3\% < \delta_{zi} = 7.5\%\).
Eq. (9) ‚Äî Endogenous Sovereign Borrowing Rate $$r_{dc,t} = (1 + D_{r,t})\left(r^f + \upsilon_g\, e^{\eta_g\left(\frac{d_t + dc_t}{y_t} - \frac{\bar{d}+\bar{dc}}{\bar{y}}\right)}\right)$$
Commercial debt rate rises with (i) disaster credit shock \(D_r\) and (ii) debt-to-GDP deviations from steady state. Follows Schmitt-Groh√© & Uribe (2003).
Eq. (10) ‚Äî Government Budget / Fiscal Gap $$\mathfrak{Gap}_t = P_t \Delta b_t + \Delta dc_t + (\tau^c_t - \tau^c_o)\,P_t c_t - \Delta\mathfrak{s}_t$$
The fiscal gap (expenditures ‚àí revenues at baseline tax) is closed by domestic borrowing, external commercial borrowing, tax adjustments, and/or drawing on the contingency fund.
Eq. (11) ‚Äî Fiscal Rule for VAT $$\tau^c_t = \tau^c_{t-1} + \lambda_1\left((\tau^c_t)^{\text{target}} - \tau^c_{t-1}\right) + \lambda_2 \frac{(P_{t-1}b_{t-1}+dc_{t-1}) - (Pb+dc)^{\text{target}}}{y_t}$$
Tax rate responds to both the target tax (if gap were fully tax-financed) and debt deviations. \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \geq 0\) control adjustment speed.

4.3 ‚Äî Households

Eq. (12) ‚Äî CES Consumption Basket $$c^i_t = \left[\rho_x^{1/\epsilon}(c^i_{x,t})^{\frac{\epsilon-1}{\epsilon}} + \rho_m^{1/\epsilon}(c^i_{m,t})^{\frac{\epsilon-1}{\epsilon}} + (1-\rho_x-\rho_m)^{1/\epsilon}(c^i_{n,t})^{\frac{\epsilon-1}{\epsilon}}\right]^{\frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon-1}}$$
For \(i \in \{\mathfrak{r}, \mathfrak{c}\}\). Three goods: domestic tradable, imported, nontradable. \(\epsilon = 0.5\) (low substitutability).
Eq. (16) ‚Äî Euler Equation (Ricardian) $$c^{\mathfrak{r}}_t = c^{\mathfrak{r}}_{t+1}\left[\beta(1+r_t)\frac{1+\tau^c_t}{1+\tau^c_{t+1}}\right]^{-\varsigma_c}$$
Standard consumption Euler with intertemporal elasticity \(\varsigma_c = 0.34\) and VAT wedge.
Eq. (19) ‚Äî Liquidity-Constrained Budget $$(1+\tau^c_t)\,P_t c^{\mathfrak{c}}_t = w_t l^{\mathfrak{c}}_t + \frac{a}{1+a}(\mathcal{R}_t + \mathcal{T}_t)$$
Hand-to-mouth: consumption = after-tax wage income + share of remittances \(\mathcal{R}\) and transfers \(\mathcal{T}\).

4.4 ‚Äî Recovery Dynamics

Eq. (22) ‚Äî Sigmoid Recovery Path $$x_t = e^{-\gamma_x t}\, x^{\text{Pam}} + (1 - e^{-\gamma_x t})\, x^{\text{pre-Pam}}$$
For \(x \in \{z^i, s, A_j, D_r\}\). Governs gradual transition from disaster state to steady state. \(\gamma_x\) controls speed ‚Äî critical parameter for your thesis on recovery dynamics.
Reconstruction Investment (derived) $$i^{\text{reconstruction}}_{zi,t} = \frac{z^i_t - (1-\delta_{zi})z^i_{t-1}}{s_t}$$
Given the target path for public capital \(z^i_t\), the required investment flow is backed out. Peaks at 5.5% of GDP.

4.5 ‚Äî Market Clearing & Balance of Payments

Eq. (20) ‚Äî Nontradable Market Clearing $$y_{n,t} = \rho_n\left(\frac{P_{n,t}}{P_t}\right)^{-\epsilon} c_t + a_k(i_{n,t}+i_{x,t}+AC_{n,t}+AC_{x,t}) + a_{zi}i_{zi,t} + a_{za}i_{za,t}$$
Nontradable output = consumption demand + nontradable content of private and public investment.
Eq. (21) ‚Äî Balance of Payments $$\underbrace{\Delta b^*_t + \Delta dc_t + \Delta d_t + \mathcal{G}_t + \mathcal{R}_t - \Delta\mathfrak{s}_t}_{\text{Capital Account}} = -\underbrace{\text{CA}_t}_{\text{Current Account}}$$
Capital inflows (borrowing + grants + remittances ‚àí savings) finance the current account deficit.

Estimation & Solution Method

‚ö†Ô∏è

No econometric estimation. This model is calibrated and solved via deterministic perfect-foresight simulation, not Bayesian estimation or GMM. The disaster is a known, one-time event ‚Äî not a stochastic process. This is a key methodological distinction from HANK papers you're replicating.

Solution Method

The model is solved as a perfect foresight transition path from the disaster-impacted state back toward the pre-disaster steady state, comparing alternative policy scenarios. The framework builds on Buffie et al. (2012) ‚Äî the IMF's Debt, Investment, Growth, and Natural Disasters (DIGNAD) model.

Calibration Strategy

  • Disaster parameters: Matched to Vanuatu's Post-Disaster Needs Assessment (60.8% GDP damage)
  • National accounts: IMF WEO, IFPRI GTAP database for factor shares
  • Debt data: IMF 2016 Debt Sustainability Analysis
  • Field assessment: Authors visited Vanuatu in late 2016 during Article IV consultations
  • Investment efficiency: \(s_o = 60\%\) from IMF PIMA/PIE-X benchmarks

üîë Core Finding

Donors must provide an additional 50% of pre-cyclone GDP in grants over 15 years to keep debt sustainable under accelerated reconstruction. This far exceeds typical post-disaster commitments and indeed actual disbursements fell short.

üí° Resilience Payoff

Pre-disaster adaptation investment (3% GDP/yr for 5 years) reduces damage to standard infrastructure by 5.2%, private capital by 9.7%, and TFP losses by ~3.9%. This cuts peak debt from >90% to <60% of GDP ‚Äî at lower total cost for donors.

Key insight for your thesis: The speed of recovery (governed by \(\gamma_x\) in Eq. 22) is a critical determinant of macroeconomic losses. Faster reconstruction reduces the persistence of output gaps but requires larger financing ‚Äî exactly the tension your RQ4 addresses. Marto et al. show that even with resilience investments, donor support remains essential for catastrophic events.
