01 Core Problem

What gap does this paper address?

The Problem

Standard DSGE models treat a country as a single homogeneous region. But in reality, fiscal policy effects are not uniform ‚Äî they depend on where the policy is implemented and which level of government (local vs. central) implements it. Existing currency-union models (Benigno 2004, Gal√≠ & Monacelli 2008) model multi-region economies but lack a central government that operates across all regions.

Key Questions the Paper Answers

‚ë† How do we construct an \(N\)-region DSGE model within a single country?
‚ë° Does the fiscal multiplier differ when local vs. central government implements policy?
‚ë¢ Does the multiplier differ depending on which region receives the spending?
‚ë£ How does a fiscal crisis (sovereign default risk) in one region spill over to other regions?

üîó Relevance for Your Thesis

Your RQ2 asks: "How can a dynamic macroeconomic model capture regional economic structure?" ‚Äî Tamegawa's framework is a direct answer. Replace "fiscal shock" with "natural disaster shock," and the two-region structure maps onto your disaster-affected vs. non-affected region setup. His treatment of interregional linkages via bankers and a common goods market is directly applicable to your interregional spillover analysis (RQ3).

02 Model Features

Anatomy of the two-region DSGE economy

The model is a real business cycle framework (no nominal rigidities) with annual frequency. The country is split into \(N\) regions (simulated with \(N=2\)). Each region has unit population, so national population = \(N\).

Six Agent Types

üè≠

Firms

Region-specific production using capital, labor, and social (public) capital. Single output good, but labor markets are segmented by region. Cobb-Douglas technology with region-specific and macro TFP shocks.

üìä

Ricardian Households

Optimizing agents who maximize intertemporal utility with external habit formation. Hold deposits, pay local and central taxes. Euler equation pins down consumption-savings decisions. Share \((1 - \omega_i - \omega_{B,i})\) of regional population.

üõí

Non-Ricardian Households

Rule-of-thumb consumers who spend all disposable income each period: \(c_t^{r,i} = w_t^i h_t^i - t_t^i - t_t^C\). Key for positive consumption multiplier. Share \(\omega_i\) of regional population (differs by region).

üè¶

Bankers

Financial intermediaries who collect deposits nationally, lend to firms (capital) and to both local & central governments (bonds). Face default risk from government bonds. Determine investment, bond pricing, and interest rate spreads.

üèõÔ∏è

Local Governments

Region-specific. Issue bonds \(B_t^i\), collect local lump-sum tax \(t_t^i\), spend on regional goods \(G_t^i\). Face a risk premium tied to their own debt level. Can default.

üáØüáµ

Central Government

Operates across all regions. Issues bonds \(B_t^c\), collects uniform central tax \(t_t^C\) from all regions, spends in targeted regions \(G_t^{C,i}\). More credible (lower default rate). Spending builds social capital in targeted region.

Tamegawa models regions as differing in production technology (Œ±, Œ∑), household composition (œâ), and fiscal credibility (v, p). For your disaster application, you can adapt this by differentiating regions along: disaster exposure (probability of destruction shock), capital vulnerability (fraction of capital destroyed), and recovery infrastructure (speed of reconstruction). 04 Complete Equation List

All 34 equations in the model, organized by agent block

A. Firms Production

Each region \(i\) produces with Cobb-Douglas technology. Social capital \(K_t^{g,i}\) enters as an externality. Labor markets are region-specific.

Eq. (1) ‚Äî Production Function
$$Y_t^i = e^{z_t^i} e^{z_t^m} \left(K_t^i\right)^{\alpha_i} \left[(1-\omega_{B,i}) h_t^i\right]^{1-\alpha_i} \left(K_t^{g,i}\right)^{\eta_i}$$
Region-specific TFP \(z_t^i\), macro TFP \(z_t^m\). Social capital productivity \(\eta_i\) varies: \(\eta_1=0.2\), \(\eta_2=0\).
Eq. (2) ‚Äî Labor Demand (FOC for labor)
$$(1-\omega_{B,i}) w_t^i = (1-\alpha_i) \frac{Y_t^i}{h_t^i}$$
Eq. (3) ‚Äî Capital Demand (FOC for capital)
$$r_t^i = \alpha_i \frac{Y_t^i}{K_t^i}$$

B. Ricardian Households Optimization

Maximize \(E_0 \sum \beta^t \{ \log(c_t^{o,i} - b \bar{c}_{t-1}^{o,i}) + \theta_i \log(1-h_t^i) \}\) subject to budget constraint. External habit: \(\bar{c}_t^{o,i} = c_t^{o,i}\).

Eq. (4) ‚Äî Budget Constraint
$$d_{t+1}^i = R_{t-1}^D d_t^i + w_t^i h_t^i - c_t^{o,i} - t_t^i - t_t^C$$
Eq. (5) ‚Äî Marginal Utility of Consumption
$$\frac{1}{c_t^{o,i} - b c_{t-1}^{o,i}} - \lambda_t^i = 0$$
Eq. (6) ‚Äî Euler Equation
$$\lambda_t^i - E_t\left(\beta R_t^D \lambda_{t+1}^i\right) = 0$$
Eq. (7) ‚Äî Labor Supply
$$\frac{\theta_i}{1 - h_t^i} - \lambda_t^i w_t^i = 0$$

C. Non-Ricardian Households Rule-of-Thumb

Consume all disposable income each period. Work and earn wages identically to Ricardian households.

Eq. (8) ‚Äî Consumption Rule
$$c_t^{r,i} = w_t^i h_t^i - t_t^i - t_t^C$$
This is the critical equation generating positive consumption response to fiscal expansion. More non-Ricardian households ‚Üí larger fiscal multiplier.

D. Bankers Financial Intermediation

Bankers are the financial core ‚Äî they link regions through national deposit collection and lending. Discount factor: \(\Lambda_{t+1,t} = \beta_B C_t^B / C_{t+1}^B\). Indicator functions \(\mathbf{1}_t^i\) and \(\mathbf{1}_t^c\) capture default events.

Eq. (9) ‚Äî Banker Budget Constraint
$$C_t^B + \sum_i B_{t+1}^i + B_{t+1}^c + R_{t-1}^d D_t + \sum_i Q_t^i K_{t+1}^i + \sum_i t_t^i + n \cdot t_t^C$$ $$= \sum_i r_t^{i,k} K_t^i + \sum_i Q_t^i \left\{(1-\delta)K_t^i + I_t^i[1-\Phi(I_t^i/I_{t-1}^i)]\right\} + D_{t+1} + \sum_i R_{t-1}^i B_t^i + R_{t-1}^c B_t^c$$
Eq. (16) ‚Äî Local Bond Pricing (No-Arbitrage)
$$1 = E_t\left[\Lambda_{t+1,t} \cdot R_t^i \cdot (1 - p_{t+1}^i)\right]$$
Default probability \(p_{t+1}^i\) creates a risk premium: higher default risk ‚Üí higher required return \(R_t^i\).
Eq. (17) ‚Äî Central Bond Pricing
$$1 = E_t\left[\Lambda_{t+1,t} \cdot R_t^c \cdot (1 - p_{t+1}^c)\right]$$
Eq. (18) ‚Äî Deposit Rate
$$1 = E_t\left[\Lambda_{t+1,t} \cdot R_t^d\right]$$
Eq. (19) ‚Äî Capital Valuation (Tobin's Q)
$$Q_t^i = E_t\left(\Lambda_{t+1,t}\left[Q_{t+1}^i(1-\delta) + r_{t+1}^i\right]\right)$$
Eq. (20) ‚Äî Investment FOC (with adjustment costs)
$$Q_t^i \left\{1 - \Phi'\left(\frac{I_t^i}{I_{t-1}^i}\right)\frac{I_t^i}{I_{t-1}^i} - \Phi\left(\frac{I_t^i}{I_{t-1}^i}\right)\right\} + E_t\left(\Lambda_{t+1,t} Q_{t+1}^i \Phi'\left(\frac{I_{t+1}^i}{I_t^i}\right)\left(\frac{I_{t+1}^i}{I_t^i}\right)^2\right) = 1$$
Adjustment cost function \(\Phi(\cdot)\) following Christiano et al. (2005). Slows capital adjustment, increasing IRF realism.

E. Aggregation Accounting

Eq. (21) ‚Äî Regional Consumption
$$C_t^i = \omega_i c_t^{r,i} + (1 - \omega_i - \omega_{B,i}) c_t^{o,i}$$
Eq. (22)‚Äì(24) ‚Äî Tax and Spending Aggregation
$$T_t^i = t_t^i, \qquad T_t^C = N \cdot t_t^C, \qquad G_t^C = \sum_{i \in \mathbf{N}} G_t^{C,i}$$

F. Government Sector Fiscal Rules

Eq. (25) ‚Äî Local Government Budget Constraint
$$B_{t+1}^i = R_{t-1}^i B_t^i + G_t^i - T_t^i$$
Eq. (26) ‚Äî Central Government Budget Constraint
$$B_{t+1}^c = R_{t-1}^c B_t^c + G_t^c - T_t^C$$
Eq. (27)‚Äì(28) ‚Äî Tax Rules (responds to debt)
$$\log t_t^i = \text{const.} + \xi_b \log B_t^i \qquad \log t_t^C = \text{const.} + \xi_b \log B_t^c$$
Tax elasticity \(\xi_b = 0.9\) ensures fiscal solvency.
Eq. (29)‚Äì(30) ‚Äî Government Spending (AR(1))
$$\log G_t^i = \text{const.} + \rho_i \log G_{t-1}^i + \varepsilon_t^i \qquad \log G_t^c = \text{const.} + \rho_c \log G_{t-1}^c + \varepsilon_t^c$$
Eq. (31)‚Äì(32) ‚Äî Default Probability (endogenous risk)
$$\log(1 - p_t^i) = \text{const.} - v_i \log B_t^i \qquad \log(1 - p_t^c) = \text{const.} - v_c \log B_t^c$$
Higher debt ‚Üí higher default probability ‚Üí higher risk premium ‚Üí higher interest rate ‚Üí crowding out. This is the fiscal crisis transmission channel.

G. Social Capital & Market Clearing Closure

Eq. (33) ‚Äî Social Capital Accumulation
$$K_{t+1}^{g,i} = (1-\delta_g) K_t^{g,i} + G_t^i + G_t^{C,i}$$
Both local and central government spending build social capital. But only Region 1 benefits (\(\eta_1 = 0.2\)).
Eq. (34) ‚Äî Goods Market Clearing
$$\sum_{i \in \mathbf{N}} Y_t^i = \sum_{i \in \mathbf{N}} \left(C_t^i + I_t^i + G_t^i + G_t^{C,i}\right) + C_t^B$$

05 Solution Method & Calibration

How the model is parameterized and solved

Method: Calibration + Log-Linearization + Dynare

This paper does not estimate parameters econometrically. Instead, it uses calibration: parameters are set to match stylized facts and steady-state ratios, then the log-linearized model is solved using Dynare for impulse response analysis.

Solution Steps

‚ë† Set parameter values (Table 1) to match steady-state ratios (C/Y = 0.6, G/Y = 0.08 for local, G/Y = 0.05 for central, B/Y = 1.0).
‚ë° Log-linearize all equations around the deterministic steady state.
‚ë¢ Solve the linearized rational expectations system in Dynare (Blanchard-Kahn conditions).
‚ë£ Compute IRFs to 1% of national output fiscal spending shocks in 4 scenarios.
‚ë§ Compare fiscal multipliers across scenarios.

No Estimation ‚Äî Why?

The paper's goal is methodological demonstration: showing regional scientists how to build a tractable multi-region DSGE. The calibration is illustrative, not fitted to any specific country's data. This is a common approach in the regional DSGE literature.

Why Case (iii) Has the Largest Multiplier

Three reinforcing factors make central-government spending in Region 1 most effective:

‚ë† Lower crowding out: The central government has \(v_c = 0\), meaning no risk premium on its bonds ‚Äî it faces no default penalty. Local Govt 1 faces \(v_1 = 0.03\), which raises interest rates and crowds out investment.

‚ë° Productive social capital: Region 1 has \(\eta_1 = 0.2\), so government spending directly increases output through \(K^{g,1}\). In Region 2, \(\eta_2 = 0\), so spending has no supply-side effect beyond the initial demand impulse.

‚ë¢ More non-Ricardian households: Region 1 has \(\omega_1 = 0.4\) vs. \(\omega_2 = 0.3\). More rule-of-thumb consumers means higher marginal propensity to consume, amplifying the demand multiplier.

Fiscal Crisis Simulation (Section 5)

An information shock \(\varepsilon_t^{p,2}\) raises expected default probability of Local Govt 2. This triggers:

‚Üí Risk premiums soar on Region 2 bonds ‚Üí interest rates rise ‚Üí by arbitrage, central and Region 1 interest rates also rise ‚Üí tax increases in Region 2 ‚Üí non-Ricardian consumption falls ‚Üí deposit rates fall (less bond supply) ‚Üí Ricardian consumption rises but hours worked and output decline nationally.

The fiscal crisis spills across regions through the financial intermediation (banker) channel ‚Äî even though Region 1's fundamentals are unchanged.

üîó Key Takeaway for Your Thesis

Interregional spillover mechanism: Tamegawa shows that fiscal shocks in one region transmit to other regions through: (a) the national goods market clearing condition (Eq. 34), (b) the banking system which pools deposits nationally and prices risk across all government bonds, and (c) the central government's uniform taxation. For your disaster model, the same channels apply ‚Äî a natural disaster destroying capital in one region will transmit to the non-affected region through these financial and goods-market linkages. You can introduce a capital destruction shock (reducing \(K_t^i\) in the affected region) and a reconstruction spending channel (analogous to government spending building social capital via Eq. 33).

What Your Model Needs Beyond Tamegawa

‚ë† Rare disaster shock: Tamegawa has fiscal shocks but no capital destruction. You need a Poisson-distributed disaster that destroys a fraction of physical capital \(K_t^i\) in the affected region ‚Äî following Barro (2006, 2009) rare disaster literature.

‚ë° Recovery dynamics: Tamegawa's social capital accumulation (Eq. 33) provides a starting point, but you need explicit reconstruction speed parameters and possibly a recovery function that depends on government policy choice (RQ1, RQ4).

‚ë¢ Policy comparison: Tamegawa compares local vs. central spending. You should compare: (a) direct transfers to affected households, (b) reconstruction investment, (c) central vs. local government funding, (d) speed of disbursement.

‚ë£ Trade structure: Tamegawa's single-good assumption is limiting. For Indonesia, regional specialization (commodities vs. manufacturing) matters. Consider a two-good extension with terms-of-trade effects.

‚ë§ Welfare analysis: Tamegawa reports multipliers but not welfare. Your thesis should compute consumption-equivalent welfare losses across regions to answer RQ3.

Limitations of Tamegawa (2012)

No labor mobility ‚Äî households cannot relocate, which is unrealistic for disaster analysis where displacement is central. Single good ‚Äî no terms of trade between regions. Calibration only ‚Äî not estimated on actual data, so multiplier magnitudes are illustrative. No nominal rigidities ‚Äî the real business cycle framework misses monetary policy interactions. No heterogeneous agents within regions ‚Äî only the Ricardian/non-Ricardian split, not a full wealth distribution (unlike HANK models you're studying).
